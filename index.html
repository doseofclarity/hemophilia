Hemophilia

"An X‑linked inherited bleeding disorder caused by deficiency of clotting factor VIII (hemophilia A) or factor IX (hemophilia B)." – Pillitteri

Hemophilia Like You Are 5 Years Old

🧸 Imagine your blood is like a big bowl of jelly that helps your cuts stop bleeding.

• In most people, the jelly has special "glue" (called clotting factors) that seals a cut quickly.
• In hemophilia, the glue is missing – or there isn't enough of it. So when a person gets a small cut, they may bleed for a long time.
• The worst bleeding happens inside the body – into knees, ankles, and elbows. That can hurt a lot and make the joints swollen.
• Boys get hemophilia more often because of the way the "glue gene" is passed down from mom.

🩺 Doctors can give special medicine (factor concentrate) that works like the missing glue, so children can run, jump, and play safely!

Why does bleeding last longer?

Missing "glue" (factor VIII/IX)

Definition & Epidemiology

Hemophilia – a group of inherited bleeding disorders characterized by deficiency of coagulation factor VIII (hemophilia A) or factor IX (hemophilia B). Hemophilia A accounts for 80‑85% of cases.

📊 Global incidence
• Hemophilia A: 1 in 5,000 male births
• Hemophilia B: 1 in 30,000 male births
• Approximately 400,000 people worldwide have hemophilia (400,000 living with the disorder)
🇵🇭 Philippines (Philippine Hemophilia Association)
• Estimated 10,000 people with hemophilia, but many remain undiagnosed.
• Only about 2,000 registered with the association.
• Access to factor concentrates remains limited; many rely on cryoprecipitate or fresh frozen plasma.
⚠️ Key point: Hemophilia is almost exclusively seen in males. Females are carriers (heterozygous) but may have mild bleeding symptoms due to lyonization (X‑inactivation).

Genetics & Types

🧬 X‑linked recessive inheritance
• Gene for factor VIII (F8) and factor IX (F9) located on X chromosome.
• Males (XY) with the mutation express the disease.
• Females (XX) are carriers (usually asymptomatic) but can have mild bleeding if X‑inactivation is skewed.
• Affected male transmits mutation to all daughters (obligate carriers), never to sons.
🔹 Types of hemophilia
• Hemophilia A (classic hemophilia) – factor VIII deficiency (80‑85%).
• Hemophilia B (Christmas disease) – factor IX deficiency (15‑20%).
• Hemophilia C (factor XI deficiency) – rare, autosomal recessive, milder bleeding, more common in Ashkenazi Jews.
📌 Pillitteri mnemonic: "A for Eight" – Hemophilia A = factor VIII deficiency. "B for Nine" – Hemophilia B = factor IX deficiency.

Clinical Features & Severity

🩸 Bleeding patterns
• Hemarthrosis (bleeding into joints) – most common, targets knees, ankles, elbows → pain, swelling, limited motion.
• Easy bruising, soft tissue hematomas.
• Mucous membrane bleeding (epistaxis, gum bleeding).
• Prolonged bleeding after circumcision, dental extraction, trauma.
• Intracranial hemorrhage (most serious, can be fatal).
📊 Severity (based on factor level)
• Severe: <1% of normal factor activity → spontaneous bleeding, hemarthrosis (1‑2 times/week).
• Moderate: 1‑5% → bleeding after minor trauma, occasional spontaneous bleeding.
• Mild: 5‑40% → bleeding only after major trauma or surgery; often diagnosed later in life.
📌 Hemarthrosis complications: Chronic synovitis → hemosiderin deposition → cartilage destruction → hemophilic arthropathy (target joint).

Diagnosis & Tests

  • Screening coagulation tests: Prolonged activated partial thromboplastin time (aPTT). Normal prothrombin time (PT) and bleeding time.
  • Factor assays (gold standard): Factor VIII and factor IX activity levels. Differentiates hemophilia A vs B.
  • von Willebrand factor (VWF) assay: Excludes von Willebrand disease (which also causes low factor VIII).
  • Genetic testing: Identifies mutation (F8 gene inversion intron 22 is common in severe hemophilia A). Used for carrier detection and prenatal diagnosis.
  • Prenatal diagnosis: Chorionic villus sampling (CVS) at 11‑14 weeks or amniocentesis after 15 weeks for at‑risk pregnancies.
🔍 Gold standard: Factor VIII or IX activity assay (one‑stage clotting assay).

Management & Replacement Therapy

💊 Factor replacement (mainstay)
• Plasma‑derived or recombinant factor VIII/IX concentrates.
• Prophylaxis (regular infusions 2‑3×/week) prevents hemarthrosis and arthropathy – standard of care for severe hemophilia.
• On‑demand therapy for bleeding episodes.
• Dose calculation: 1 IU/kg increases factor VIII level by 2% (for hemophilia A). For hemophilia B: 1 IU/kg increases factor IX by 1%.
💊 Other agents
• Desmopressin (DDAVP) – releases endogenous factor VIII; effective only in mild hemophilia A (not B).
• Antifibrinolytics (tranexamic acid, epsilon‑aminocaproic acid) – for mucosal bleeding (oral, dental) as adjunct.
• Emicizumab (Hemlibra) – bispecific antibody that mimics factor VIII, for hemophilia A with or without inhibitors.
• Recombinant factor VIIa (rFVIIa) – for hemophilia with inhibitors.
📌 Pillitteri note: "Avoid intramuscular injections, aspirin, and NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) because they increase bleeding risk. Acetaminophen is safe for pain/fever."

Nursing Care & Complications

👩‍⚕️ Nursing interventions
• Bleeding precautions: soft toothbrush, avoid IM injections, use small‑gauge needles, apply pressure for 5‑10 minutes after venipuncture.
• For hemarthrosis: RICE (Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation) plus factor replacement.
• Pre‑surgical / dental factor coverage.
• Administer vaccines subcutaneously (not IM).
• Educate family about home infusion therapy.
⚠️ Complications
• Inhibitor development (alloantibodies against factor VIII/IX) – occurs in 20‑30% of severe hemophilia A, renders replacement ineffective.
• Hemophilic arthropathy (chronic joint disease).
• Transfusion‑transmitted infections (hepatitis B/C, HIV) – rare now with viral inactivation and recombinant products.
• Intracranial hemorrhage (leading cause of death in severe hemophilia).
📞 Emergency signs (teach parents): Headache, vomiting, lethargy, unequal pupils → possible intracranial bleed. Neck pain, difficulty swallowing → retropharyngeal bleed.

Prevention & Genetic Counseling

  • Carrier testing: Female relatives of affected males can undergo factor assay and genetic testing (F8/F9 mutation analysis).
  • Prenatal diagnosis: Chorionic villus sampling (CVS) at 11‑14 weeks or amniocentesis after 15 weeks to determine fetal sex and mutation status.
  • Preimplantation genetic diagnosis (PGD): For couples undergoing in vitro fertilization.
  • Primary prevention of bleeding: Regular prophylaxis with factor concentrates starting in early childhood prevents joint damage and allows normal physical activity.
📌 Pillitteri patient teaching: "Young boys with hemophilia should be allowed to play, but contact sports (football, boxing, wrestling) should be avoided. Swimming, walking, and biking with helmet are recommended."

Flashcards & Mnemonics

Q: Most common type of hemophilia?
Hemophilia A (factor VIII deficiency) – 80‑85% of cases.
Q: Inheritance pattern of hemophilia A and B?
X‑linked recessive.
Q: Drug of choice for mild hemophilia A?
Desmopressin (DDAVP) – releases endogenous factor VIII.
Q: Which analgesic is safe for pain in hemophilia?
Acetaminophen (paracetamol). Avoid aspirin and NSAIDs.
Q: Most common bleeding site in severe hemophilia?
Joints (hemarthrosis) – knees, ankles, elbows.
Q: What test is prolonged in hemophilia?
Activated partial thromboplastin time (aPTT). PT is normal.
🧠 Mnemonics
“A for Eight, B for Nine” – Hemophilia A = factor VIII deficiency; Hemophilia B = factor IX deficiency.
“F.E.A.R.” – Factor deficiency, Easy bleeding, Absent (normal) PT, aPTT prolonged.
“R.I.C.E.” – Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation for hemarthrosis.
“C.A.R.E.” – Concentrate replacement, Avoid IM injection, Rest joints, Educate family.
